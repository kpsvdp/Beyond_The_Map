The complete story

Some trips begin with a carefully written plan. This one began with a long route across Central Europe and a simple goal: to see how much the atmosphere could change from one country to the next.

Some trips begin with a carefully written plan. This one began with a long route across Central Europe and a simple goal: to see how much the atmosphere could change as I moved from one country to the next.

My journey started in Newcastle and continued through Poland, Czechia, Austria, Slovakia, Hungary and Slovenia. I travelled by plane, train, coach, tram, metro and on foot. Some days were calm sightseeing days. Others were spent watching unfamiliar landscapes pass outside a train window.

It was a fast journey, but every city gave me a different memory.

Route

Poland: From Krakow's Old Streets to the Mountains of Zakopane

Country chapter

Day 1: Newcastle to Krakow

I left Newcastle in the morning and flew to Amsterdam for my connection to Krakow. The journey was on one ticket, so the transfer at Amsterdam Airport was straightforward. After finding the next gate and waiting for the second flight, I finally arrived in Krakow in the late afternoon.

Instead of taking an expensive taxi, I used the airport train to reach Krakow Glowny. The journey into the city was easy, and the main station placed me close to the historic centre.

After checking into my hostel and leaving my bag, I went outside for my first walk through Krakow. Rynek Glowny was full of old buildings, lights and people moving between cafes and restaurants. I walked around the Cloth Hall and continued towards St Mary's Basilica.

I had expected to feel tired after two flights, but arriving in a new city gave me fresh energy. For dinner, I found a small local restaurant and ordered pierogi. It was a simple first meal, but it felt like the proper beginning of the trip.

Day 2: Krakow to Zakopane

The following morning, I returned to the Old Town and walked towards Wawel Hill. From there, I could see the Vistula River and part of the city below. I explored the castle grounds before continuing to Kazimierz, a neighbourhood with historic streets, small shops and cafes.

At lunchtime, I chose a local milk-bar-style restaurant. The food was filling, affordable and much better suited to the trip than eating beside the busiest tourist square.

Later, I collected my luggage and walked to Krakow MDA Bus Station. My coach to Zakopane left in the evening. As Krakow disappeared behind us, the roads gradually became quieter and the landscape began to change.

I arrived at Zakopane Bus Station after dark and made my way to Willa Pod Bandera, where I stayed for two nights. After checking in, I walked along Krupowki Street. The centre felt completely different from Krakow. The buildings, food stalls and cold mountain air made it clear that I had entered the Tatra region.

I tried oscypek, the smoked mountain cheese for which the area is known, before returning to the room.

Day 3: A Full Day in Zakopane

Zakopane was the first place on the journey where I slowed down.

I started the morning in the town centre and then travelled towards Gubalowka. The ride up gave me wide views across Zakopane and towards the mountains. The weather was cold, but the view made the journey worthwhile.

After returning to the centre, I stopped for potato pancakes and hot tea. The meal was exactly what I needed after spending time outside.

There were several ways I could have used the rest of the day. A trip to Morskie Oko was tempting, but mountain conditions and limited winter daylight made it a serious plan rather than a casual walk. I chose a safer local experience and spent more time exploring the town and enjoying the mountain atmosphere.

That evening, I returned to Krupowki Street for dinner. Zakopane was busy, but the side streets around it were much quieter. Back at Willa Pod Bandera, I packed my bag because the next day would be one of the longest travel days of the entire trip.

Route

Czechia: A Short but Memorable Stay in Prague

Country chapter

Day 4: Zakopane to Prague

I left Zakopane early by coach and returned to Krakow. I had deliberately kept a long gap before my international train because traffic from the mountains can be unpredictable.

After reaching Krakow, I walked to the railway station, bought food for the journey and checked the departure board carefully. My route to Prague included a change at Ostrava. It was a long journey, but train travel made it comfortable. I could sit back, watch the Polish countryside pass and then notice the gradual change as the train entered Czechia.

I arrived at Prague hlavni nadrazi in the evening. From the station, I used local transport to reach Globe Hostel Prague Adults Only. After checking in, I went for a short walk, but I did not try to see everything immediately. Prague deserved an early start the next morning.

Day 5: Prague Before the Train to Vienna

I began at Charles Bridge while the city was still relatively quiet. Walking across the bridge early made a huge difference. I could see the statues, the river and Prague Castle without fighting through the heaviest crowds.

From Mala Strana, I climbed towards Prague Castle. The streets became steeper, but every turn gave me another view over the red rooftops. I walked through the castle area and then returned towards the Old Town.

By lunchtime, I had reached Old Town Square and the Astronomical Clock. Prague was beautiful, but my time there was limited. I found a cafe away from the busiest part of the square, ate a quick lunch and collected my luggage.

In the afternoon, I boarded the train from Prague to Vienna. Leaving Prague after only one night felt rushed, but the rail journey was comfortable and direct. By the time I arrived at Vienna Hauptbahnhof, the city was already dark.

My room was at a&o Wien Hauptbahnhof, close to the station. That location made arrival easy and would also help when I left for Bratislava.

Route

Austria: Palaces, Trams and Vienna Coffee

Country chapter

Day 6: A Full Day in Vienna

Vienna felt more formal than the cities I had already visited. The streets were wide, the public transport was organised and grand buildings seemed to appear everywhere.

I began at Schonbrunn Palace. Rather than trying to visit every paid section, I chose the main experience and spent time walking through the grounds. From there, I used Vienna's underground and tram network to reach the Belvedere area.

In the afternoon, I continued to the historic centre and St Stephen's Cathedral. I walked through the surrounding streets and followed part of the Ringstrasse, passing some of Vienna's most impressive architecture.

Food was more expensive here than in Krakow or Bratislava, so I balanced one traditional meal with simpler bakery and supermarket food. I stopped for apple strudel and coffee during the afternoon. It was a small break, but it felt like one of the most Viennese moments of the trip.

That evening, I returned to the hostel near Hauptbahnhof and prepared for another early departure.

Route

Slovakia: One Day in Compact Bratislava

Country chapter

Day 7: Vienna to Bratislava

I left Vienna early and travelled to Bratislava. The journey between the two capitals was short, which made the change of country feel almost too easy.

After arriving, I went to Patio Hostel, left my bag and started walking. Bratislava's centre was much smaller than Vienna's, and most of the main places could be reached on foot.

I began with the Blue Church before heading towards the Old Town. I walked through narrow streets, passed Michael's Gate and stopped in one of the squares for lunch. I tried bryndzove halusky, one of Slovakia's best-known dishes.

Later, I climbed towards Bratislava Castle. The castle grounds offered wide views across the Danube and towards the modern parts of the city. Because the city was compact, the day never felt rushed.

In the evening, I returned to the Old Town and then walked back to Patio Hostel. Bratislava had been a short stop, but it worked perfectly between Vienna and Budapest.

Route

Hungary: Budapest on Both Sides of the Danube

Country chapter

Day 8: Bratislava to Budapest

I had most of the morning free in Bratislava before collecting my bag and travelling to the railway station. My train continued south to Budapest.

I arrived at Budapest Nyugati in the evening and used the city's public transport to reach Vitae Hostel. Budapest immediately felt larger and busier than Bratislava. Trams crossed the city, metro stations connected the different districts and the Danube divided Buda from Pest.

After checking in, I went out for a first evening walk. The illuminated Parliament building and the reflections on the river were more impressive than I had expected. I ended the evening with langos from a casual food place before returning to the hostel.

Day 9: Exploring Budapest

I began the day on the Buda side of the river. I walked through the Castle District and continued to Fisherman's Bastion. From there, I could see Parliament across the Danube and much of the Pest side of the city.

I continued past Matthias Church and walked down towards Chain Bridge. Crossing the bridge on foot gave me another view of both sides of Budapest.

Later, I visited the riverfront near Parliament and used the tram to cover more of the city. Budapest's transport system made it possible to move between distant sights without wasting too much time.

In the afternoon, I visited a thermal bath. After several long travel days, spending a few hours in the warm pools was exactly what I needed. I carried my own swimwear and checked the locker rules before entering, which saved time and avoided extra rental costs.

For dinner, I found a small restaurant away from the busiest tourist streets. Budapest was one of the places where I could easily have stayed longer, but Slovenia was next.

Route

Slovenia: A Green and Relaxed Ljubljana

Country chapter

Day 10: Budapest to Ljubljana

I took a direct coach from Budapest to Ljubljana. The journey was long, but avoiding unnecessary train changes made it simpler. I kept water and food in my small bag and used the time to rest.

When I arrived in Ljubljana, the difference was immediate. The city felt smaller, quieter and greener than Budapest. After checking into my hostel, I walked towards Preseren Square and Triple Bridge.

The Ljubljanica River ran through the centre, with cafes and colourful buildings along both sides. I followed the river towards Dragon Bridge and then returned through the Old Town.

For dinner, I tried struklji and finished with a slice of potica. Ljubljana did not have the grand scale of Vienna or Budapest, but its relaxed atmosphere made it one of the easiest cities to enjoy.

Day 11: Ljubljana Castle and the Old Town

I started at Central Market before walking through the historic centre. The city was compact enough that I rarely needed public transport.

Later, I travelled up to Ljubljana Castle. It was possible to walk, but the funicular saved time. From the top, I could see the rooftops, surrounding hills and the mountains in the distance.

After returning to the city, I crossed Cobblers' Bridge and walked towards Tivoli Park. The pace of the day was much calmer than Prague or Vienna. I had time to sit beside the river, drink coffee and watch the city without checking the clock every few minutes.

In the evening, I walked through Metelkova before returning to the hostel. It was my final full sightseeing day of the trip.

Route

Returning Through Budapest

Country chapter

Day 12: Ljubljana Back to Budapest

I left Ljubljana by coach and returned to Budapest. This was not the most exciting part of the journey, but it was an important decision. I had chosen to spend one final night in Budapest instead of travelling from Ljubljana and attempting to catch a flight on the same day.

That extra night protected the journey from road delays and removed the stress of a fragile connection.

After arriving, I checked into a hostel with a straightforward airport connection. I bought a simple dinner, reorganised my bag and saved offline copies of my flight details.

Day 13: Budapest to Newcastle

On the final morning, I used Budapest's airport transport and reached the terminal with enough time for security and boarding.

As the flight left Hungary, I thought about how quickly the trip had changed from one place to another: Krakow's old square, Zakopane's mountain air, Prague's bridges, Vienna's palaces, Bratislava's quiet streets, Budapest's river views and Ljubljana's relaxed centre.

The journey had included two flights, several trains, long coach rides, hostel check-ins, station transfers and more walking than I had expected. It was not a relaxing holiday in the traditional sense. The route was fast, and careful timing mattered every day.

But that was also what made it memorable.

I did not return with one favourite country. I returned with a collection of completely different moments from six of them.

What I Learned From the Journey

Country chapter

The biggest lesson was that a multi-country trip works only when the travel days are treated as part of the experience. A train through the countryside or a coach into the mountains was not lost time. It was the connection between each chapter of the journey.

I also learned that staying close to major stations can be more useful than choosing the most beautiful hostel location. In Prague, Vienna and Budapest, easy station access saved time and reduced stress.

Finally, I learned not to make the final connection too tight. Returning to Budapest one day before the flight home was less exciting than adding another attraction, but it was one of the smartest decisions in the entire plan.

Central Europe gave me historic cities, mountain scenery, efficient rail journeys, comforting food and the feeling of crossing into a new world every few days. It was a demanding route, but I would remember every part of it.
